What is Disease Progression Modelling?

This research area encounters how a disease changes over time as it progresses and the treatments effects on it. It uses statistical models such as the Hidden Markov Model to monitor how slow progressing diseases such as Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s develop over time.

The process uses pattern recognition and simulations to recognise the trends in the disease and create models. These models can then be used to predict what stage a patient is in, and how the disease will progress in them.

Applications of Disease Proression Modelling

One of the main applications is in the fields of Cancer and Tumour progression as well as HIV. The research is focused on using image analysis and machine learning to create models and graphs of data that changes and can be compared such as the set of genetic events that have taken place.

These models are then used to diagnose the disease, study individual cases and provide appropriate therapy.

Obstacles

The main obstacle keeping us from progressing in this field is data. This is because the diseases we loook at usually progress slowly, meaning that models require data from multiple sources collected over etended time periods. Time is therefore a critical factor in the progression of the field as a whole. Solutions to this obstacle include collaborative research, data sharing and database standardization, which in recent years we have seen notable effective collaborations between industry and regulatory agencies in the area of ALzheimer's disease modeling and simulation.

Recent Developments

Integration of Disease Progression Modeling with Cost-Effectiveness Analysis

Thinking about the rising cost of healthcare has mode researchers target cost-effectiveness analysis as an increasinbly important component of assessment. At current times cost-efffectiveness analyses are ususally based upon empirical models of progression like markov models, rather than theoretical models. The latter would help as it would mean we wouldn't have to watch the disease progress in someone to identify the most cost-effective solution.
Reference
In recent research by Van Hasselt et al. a cost model was included only to demonstrate proof of concept, and thus was fairly simple. This model however could be easily expanded to cover additional costs such as follow-up hospital visits and background mortality rates. Using their new framework they simulated scenarios with different treatment protocols and patient characteristics. The impact of these changes were then evaluated with on expected efficacy and cost effectiveness. This could help lead to cheaper, and more effective treatments.
